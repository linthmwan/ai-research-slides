ImageDoctor:
Diagnosing Text-to-Image Generation via Grounded Image Reasoning

Yuxiang Guo*, Jiang Liu*, Ze Wang, Hao Chen, Ximeng Sun, Yang Zhao, Jialian Wu, Xiaodong Yu, Zicheng Liu, Emad Barsoum · JHU / AMD
ICLR 2026 PosterarXiv:2510.01010OpenReview accepted

A VLM evaluator that returns four scores plus two spatial heatmaps, then turns those heatmaps into dense rewards for T2I RL.

Sources: title page p.1; OpenReview decision; arXiv:2510.01010
TL;DR

Single scalar reward is too blunt for modern T2I failures.

ImageDoctor asks the evaluator to look for local flaws, think about them, and predict multi-aspect scores plus heatmaps.

Abstract p.1; Fig.1 p.2; §3.2 p.4
Problem

Where scalar preference models break

  • Generated images often fail locally: one missing color, one extra limb, one implausible object region.
  • HPS / ImageReward / PickScore mostly compress the image into one scalar, so different failure modes can receive similar scores.
  • Once the evaluator becomes a verifier or RL reward, the missing localization becomes an optimization problem, not just an interpretability problem.
A scalar can say “bad image”; it cannot say “this patch caused the penalty.”
§1 pp.1-2; Related Work §2.2 p.3
Contribution map

ImageDoctor adds diagnostic structure to T2I evaluation

  1. Four-dimensional scoring: semantic alignment, aesthetics, plausibility, overall quality.
  2. Two heatmaps: artifact and misalignment localization.
  3. Look-think-predict: boxes first, grounded reasoning second, final evaluation last.
  4. DenseFlow-GRPO: reuse heatmaps as dense pixel-level rewards for generator RL.

Method · training

Cold start teaches the format; GRPO sharpens the reasoning

  1. Cold start stage 1: direct score prediction on image evaluation.
  2. Cold start stage 2: supervised grounded CoT with flaw boxes, reasoning traces, scores, and heatmaps.
  3. Reinforcement finetuning: GRPO over candidate responses, with verifiable rewards for grounding, score accuracy, and heatmap quality.
L = -Σ log ptheta(zi | z<i, I, P) + Σd ||Hd - H_hatd||2
§3.2.1-3.2.2 pp.5-6; Eq.(1) p.5
Reward design

The evaluator is optimized for three verifiable behaviors

R = R_G + R_S + R_H
  • Grounding reward R_G: boxes should be complete, compact, and non-redundant over flaw regions.
  • Score reward R_S: predicted scores should match human scores by L1 distance.
  • Heatmap reward R_H: predicted heatmaps should match annotated maps by L2 distance.
§3.2.2 pp.5-6
DenseFlow-GRPO

Turning diagnosis into generator supervision

  • Standard Flow-GRPO treats every pixel equally because reward is image-level.
  • ImageDoctor supplies a dense reward: global image score plus pixel-level penalty / credit from heatmaps.
  • The objective uses a pixel-wise surrogate ratio so local advantages can backpropagate to local image regions.
R_D(x0, c, h, w) = R(x0, c) + R_P(x0, c, h, w)
§4 p.6; Eq.(4)-Eq.(6) p.6
Mechanism

The real trick is changing the reward from a label to a map.

Scalar reward pressures the whole image; dense reward pressures the flawed region. That is why the method can attack local artifacts instead of merely ranking samples.

§4 p.6; §6.2 p.9
Experiments · setup

Evaluation is anchored on human preference + localization

  • RichHF-18K: 16K train, 1K val, 1K test; four scores and two heatmaps annotated by 27 annotators.
  • Generalization: GenAI-Bench and TIFA without fine-tuning on those benchmarks.
  • Metrics: PLCC / SRCC for scores; MSE, CC, KLD, SIM for heatmaps.
  • Backbone: Qwen2.5-VL-3B; four AMD MI250 GPUs; 512 x 512 training images.

Limitations

Where this could still break in production

  • Annotation bottleneck: multi-aspect scores plus heatmaps are expensive; RichHF-18K is limited in scale and recency.
  • Subjectivity: annotators disagree on image preference and exact flaw regions; misalignment heatmaps are noisier than artifact heatmaps.
  • Backbone / latency: a Qwen2.5-VL-style reasoning evaluator is heavier than scalar CLIP-like reward models.
  • Reward hacking: dense reward helps, but an optimizing generator may still exploit evaluator blind spots.
Limitations p.19; OpenReview reviews
Engineering read

What I would borrow for image-generation systems

  1. Use VLM evaluators to generate diagnostic objects, not only scalar reward.
  2. Separate semantic, aesthetic, and plausibility channels before aggregating.
  3. Store heatmaps/boxes as training artifacts; they become debuggable reward traces.
  4. Treat evaluator speed and reward hacking as first-class product constraints, not paper footnotes.
